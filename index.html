Background

About Me

I study how learning-enabled systems behave when deployed in real-world environments where data, context, and coordination structures evolve. My work sits at the boundary between control theory, machine learning, and systems engineering — asking not just whether an AI system works in the lab, but whether it remains reliable, safe, and interpretable in physical deployment.

My PhD at IISc examined situation awareness as a distributed socio-technical capability in complex operational environments. Through 7 sequential empirical studies, I developed the SMART framework and demonstrated that structured, narrative-driven information sharing measurably outperforms data-volume-heavy alternatives for team coordination.

This empirical foundation now drives a forward research agenda toward cognitive, autonomous, and self-adaptive cyber-physical systems — infrastructure that learns, adapts, and coordinates without violating physical safety constraints. I am seeking an Assistant Professor position in EE/ECE where I can build this research programme and teach the next generation of systems engineers.

PhD Work

Thesis & Key Findings

THESIS

Human-Centred Multidimensional Situation Awareness in Construction Management

Traditional situation awareness (SA) models were built for single-user, high-tempo environments like aviation. Construction is fundamentally different — fragmented teams, long-duration projects, no single actor holding a complete view, and decisions spanning Cloud → Fog → Edge levels. This thesis reconceives SA as a distributed socio-technical capability, validated through 7 sequential empirical studies and a novel framework.

Advisor: Prof. Vishal Singh IISc Bengaluru · 2020–2026 7 Sequential Studies

THE SMART FRAMEWORK — 6-DIMENSION MODEL

Cognition–Action

Perceive · Comprehend · Project · Act — the cognitive cycle adapted for distributed teams

Operational

Cloud / Fog / Edge hierarchy — tiered decision-making across system layers

Structural

Self-reporting vs. dedicated monitoring — how information is generated and owned

Systems

Human + Software + Hardware + Analog — the full socio-technical stack

Sharing

Text, visuals, video & narrative protocols — modality matters for awareness quality

Performance

Rework ↓ · Decisions faster · Safety ↑ — measurable operational outcomes

KEY FINDINGS

THEORETICAL

SA is a Distributed System

Awareness emerges from interactions between people, technology, and routines — not from a single manager's mental model. The field's foundational assumption was wrong for construction contexts.

NON-OBVIOUS

Partial SA Cycles are Functional

Fragmented awareness is not a defect. One person perceives a risk, another interprets it, a third acts — this deliberate division of the cognitive cycle is effective and efficient in construction settings.

PRACTICAL

Alignment > Information Volume

SA quality depends on how information is structured and narrated, not on total data volume. Narrative sense-making scaffolds team alignment more effectively than dashboards or data dumps.

PRACTICAL

Proactive Over Retrospective

Clarifying monitoring roles and enforcing regular broadcast cadences shifts organisations from fixing problems after the fact to intervening before they escalate. Structure enables proactivity.

HEADLINE RESULT

Structured narrative text broadcasts measurably outperformed imagery-only updates for shared situational accuracy — validated across lab simulations and live field deployments.

Research Vision

Toward Living Infrastructure

Traditional CPS were rigid: fixed sensors, hard-coded control laws, static networks. The convergence of massive IoT, physics-informed AI, and embodied robotics demands something fundamentally different — systems that learn, reconfigure, and coordinate autonomously while maintaining safety guarantees.

CORE RESEARCH QUESTION How do we orchestrate pervasive IoT, embodied robotics, and physics-informed AI to create large-scale, self-adaptive cyber-physical systems that autonomously evolve in unstructured environments — without violating safety, stability, or deterministic performance?
T1

Pervasive IoT & Edge Intelligence

Self-organizing IoT architectures with decentralized edge intelligence. Federated learning across resource-constrained devices; dynamic topology reconfiguration for massive, mobile IoT deployments.

T2

Embodied CPS & Distributed Robotics

Cooperative control for heterogeneous multi-agent robotic swarms. MARL constrained by Lyapunov stability; distributed consensus under severe communication delays.

T3

Physics-Informed ML & Cognitive AI

Cognitive AI models capable of safe, zero-shot adaptation. PINNs embedding power-flow equations into DNNs; neuro-symbolic AI for interpretable, physics-respecting decision-making.

T4

Generative Digital Twins

Generative, stochastic digital twins for predictive system evolution. GANs and diffusion models for edge-case scenario synthesis; closing the sim-to-real gap for continuous learning.

T5

Verifiable Autonomy & Safety

Mathematical verification and robust safety envelopes for AI-driven CPS. Reachability analysis for neural-network-controlled systems; formal verification of learning-enabled robotic controllers.

Teaching

Teaching Philosophy & Portfolio

Engineering is ultimately a practice — learned by doing, by failing safely, and by seeing the gap between theory and the behaviour of real systems. Three convictions guide how I teach.

⚙️

Foundations Over Tools

Students who understand why a control loop is designed the way it is will adapt to new hardware throughout their careers. I teach from first principles; tools illuminate, they don't substitute.

🔬

Break It Deliberately

Every course includes structured experiments where students find the conditions under which a system fails. This builds the diagnostic instinct that separates good engineers from merely competent ones.

🔄

Research ↔ Teaching

Open problems from my research enter the classroom as motivation; student observations return to the lab as data. This makes courses feel alive — the field is not finished.
